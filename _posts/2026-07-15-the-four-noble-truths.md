I think first of all, we had a deeper understanding of pain and suffering. The teacher Lauren quoted Buddha that we cannot avoid pain because pain is universal, and the universe does not optimize its operation or the laws of physics to alleviate human pain. However, Buddha said that we can reduce suffering. In my understanding, suffering is our perception and reaction to pain. My finger gets bitten; it becomes suffering if I fixate on it all day long. It's certainly painful to break up with an ex. It then becomes suffering if one carries that heartbroken feeling all day long. Not only does pain become suffering, but suffering becomes torture. This is the first noble truth — that suffering exists.

<!--more-->

Why do we suffer? According to Buddha, the second noble truth says that we suffer because we attach and cling — either to material or idealistic things. For material goods, I always wanted a better phone, when in fact my phone is pretty good at the moment. The longing or clinging to a new phone is a form of suffering, because in reality I don't have it, and that gap causes me to constantly think about it, watching YouTube videos and things. For immaterial ideas, I cling to good health, cling to gatherings of friends and family. But in reality, we meet more and gather less. In addition, I often cling to an ideal form of myself — always intelligent, charming, energetic. But no. In reality, I am just some lazy guy.

Recently, I have noticed that being lazy is a form of attachment. If I am lazy and procrastinating, I am attached to my current comforts: physical comforts like the sofa and the chair, and psychological comforts of avoiding risks, not wanting to accept judgment. And the last one is actually a big one.

The third and last noble truth is the ending of suffering. The key is to let go of these idiosyncrasies. When a craving for a new phone arises, take a moment to pause and take a deep breath — and don't react to it. One small-group participant said that non-reaction to one craving probably does not make the craving go away; but it does help to loosen the grip of that craving, and over many, many tries, we can eventually detach from it. And this is the way to let go.

Every time we try to loosen the grip, we can feel a sense of freedom. That is true freedom — freedom from our own cravings.
